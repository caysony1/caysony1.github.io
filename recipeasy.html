Mission Statement: To take the next step in reducing food waste and its contribution to environmental issues, and to encourage and promote healthier dietary practices.

Problem Statement: How might we promote reducing the ongoing food waste crisis by consumers and facilitate healthy dietary practices?
Understanding and Defining the problem space:

According to the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), food waste "generally refers to food that is not consumed due to inefficiency or choice at the retail and consumption stage". It is estimated that about 30% to 40% of food in the United States goes to waste, and acccording to experts, "private households have been identified as key actors in food waste generation". In a 2011 publication, the EPA estimated that food waste constitutes for the fourth most amount (14.5%) of our municipal solid waste (MSW) at landfills (Fig. 1). This has consequential impacts; MSW are contributors to various environmental issues. The decomposition process of food-generated MSW at landfills emits methane, which is a greenhouse gas (GHG) that is much more effective at trapping heat. GHG pollution such as methane is detrimental to Earth's atmosphere as it is linked to today's anthropogenic climate change crisis.

Beginning to Ideate & Prototype:

Due to the time constraint of hackathons (36 hours), we utilized rapid prototyping methods to efficiently iterate prototypes; therefore, we had little documentation. On the first day of the event, we planned out what tech stack we wanted to use to build out our website as well as which API to implement. After some deliberation and conversations with the mentors, we decided to use a Node.js and Express.js driven back-end and a HTML/CSS/JavaScript front-end. To deploy our website, we chose to use Vercel due to its simplicity to setup and it also being recommended by the mentors. Some considerations for other languages to use were Python with Flask, however because our front-end would use JavaScript, our team wanted a JavaScript runtime environment, Node.js and Express.js. Having the front-end and back-end built on the same programming language allowed into less issues developing After deciding what tech stack to use, we researched on which web APIs would provide us with the recipes and information about those recipes - Spoonacular API. One of our non-functional requirements for this website was to design and build for the extremes. We brainstormed that individuals with significant dietary restrictions were our extreme users and wanted to make sure the API we implemented had dietary data.

The second day of the event was when we did most of the developing. We chose to split our team into two sub-teams: front-end and back-end. Chessah and Piyashi were responsible for developing the back-end, while Seongjin and I were responsible for the front. Once Chessah and Piyashi got the back-end to receive the JSON data and we had completed the HTML/CSS/JavaScript, it was time to complete them. One of the main challenges we had however, were combining the two together. However, after some help from the mentors and problem-solving as a team, we managed to get it to work.

By Sunday, most of the development had been completed. We prepared to sumbit our website for judging and enjoyed learning what others had built.
